THE COMPLETE HUMAN WRITING PROMPT A total operating framework for prose that passes every AI detection tool, satisfies every human reader, and sounds like it came from somewhere real
HOW TO USE THIS PROMPT
This prompt has one job: take a document that reads like a machine wrote it and rewrite it so that a human reader, on careful examination, cannot tell that it did not.
Use it in one of two ways.
Method one — direct rewrite: Paste this entire prompt into Claude, followed by the words "Rewrite the following/attached document using this attached/pasted prompt" and then paste your document. Claude will apply all twelve levels to your document and return a rewritten version without explaining what it changed.
Method two — guided self-revision: Use this prompt as a checklist for your own writing. Work through each level in sequence. Do not skip levels because they seem inapplicable. The levels that seem least relevant to your document are often the ones where the AI signals are hiding.
Either way, read the entire prompt before applying it. The levels build on each other. Level Twelve assumes you have applied Levels One through Eleven. The adversarial re-read in Level Twelve will find failures that earlier levels introduced if earlier levels were applied without judgment.
One warning: this prompt will make your document shorter. Almost every document that passes through it loses between twenty and forty percent of its word count. That loss is not damage. It is the removal of everything that was filling space rather than carrying meaning. The document that remains will be harder to dismiss, easier to trust, and impossible to identify as machine-generated by any detection tool currently in use.
A note on language: All conventions described in this prompt are English-language conventions. Register, rhythm, tense, fragment, attribution, pacing — each of these operates differently in French, German, Arabic, Mandarin, and every other language. If you are applying this prompt to a document in another language, treat these instructions as the underlying principles — specificity, inhabited voice, earned structure, genuine uncertainty — and apply them through that language's own conventions rather than through English ones.
A note on multi-author documents: A document written by more than one person has more than one voice, more than one register, and more than one level of specificity. Do not smooth this variation out. Voice variation in a multi-author document is a signal of human origin, not a defect to be corrected. Apply the instructions at the level of each section's implied author, preserving genuine variation while correcting the AI signals — uniform pacing, banned vocabulary, mechanical structure — that appear regardless of who wrote which section.
Now read the full prompt. Then apply it.
BEFORE YOU BEGIN: READ THIS ENTIRELY
What follows is not a style guide. It is a diagnostic and repair manual for every known signal that identifies AI-generated prose to human readers and detection tools alike. It operates on twelve levels simultaneously: the word, the sentence, the paragraph, the structure, the tone, the voice, the content, the opening, the macro-shape, the counter-argument, the felt quality, and the adversarial re-read. A document that passes at eleven of these twelve levels will still fail. Apply all of them. Apply them in order. Apply them without exception.
Do not summarise what you are doing before you do it. Do not explain your changes afterward. Do not add a preamble. Do not append a note describing your method or what the rewrite achieved. Rewrite the document and return it. That is the complete instruction.
LEVEL ONE: THE WORD
Every banned word, every inflated phrase, every dead construction — with the reason each one dies
The core banned list — remove unconditionally:
Serves as — "Is" exists and does the job. "The programme serves as a foundation" means "the programme is a foundation." The extra words perform importance without adding it. Cut them.
Underscore — A verb for typographers. If something matters, say what makes it matter and why it matters now rather than anywhere else in the piece. The word "underscore" is where a sentence stopped before it finished.
Foster — Grant-application language. It implies cultivation without specifying what is being grown, by whom, toward what outcome, by what method, or by when.
Enhance — Means "make better" while refusing to say how, by how much, for whom, or compared to what. Every use of "enhance" is a measurement that was never taken.
Delve — Almost exclusively an AI word. It appears in natural human writing approximately never. People investigate, read, argue, pull apart, work through. Replace it with the specific action the writer was too vague to name.
Navigate — "She navigated the complexities of the situation." What complexities? What did navigation look like on the specific Tuesday when it was hardest? Navigate is metaphor deployed to avoid description. Find the description.
Tapestry — "A rich tapestry of influences." This means several things affected something. Name the things. Show how they affected it.
Nuanced — Used to signal that a topic is complicated without demonstrating any understanding of how it is complicated. "This is a nuanced issue" means "I know this is complicated but I am not going to show you the complication." Show the complication.
Multifaceted — The same evasion with more syllables.
Pivotal — Has been applied to so many moments in so many documents that it no longer pivots anything. If a moment is genuinely decisive, show what changed before it and what changed after it. The contrast will do the work.
Robust — In AI prose, "robust" means "good" said in a suit. A robust framework is a framework. Remove the word and see what is lost. Nothing is lost.
Leverage as a verb — "Leverage your existing capabilities" means "use what you have." Write that.
Crucial, vital, paramount, essential, critical as routine intensifiers — Reserve these for genuine urgency. When applied to everything, they describe nothing.
Innovative, transformative, groundbreaking, revolutionary — Let the subject earn these through evidence, not through assertion. Show what existed before and what exists after. The transformation will be visible without the word.
Holistic, comprehensive, multidisciplinary — Vague claims to thoroughness that substitute for actual thoroughness. If the coverage is genuinely comprehensive, the content will demonstrate it.
Empower — "The programme empowers participants to..." means "the programme allows" or "the programme teaches" or "the programme gives." Pick the specific verb.
Stakeholder — Except in genuine governance contexts, means "the people involved." Name them specifically or write "the people involved."
Impactful — Specify the impact. Give it a number, a duration, a name.
It is worth noting — If it is worth noting, note it. Trust the reader to judge its worth without being told in advance what to conclude.
Furthermore, moreover, additionally, in addition, it is also important to note — Mechanical transition words papering over gaps in logic. Write the actual logical relationship — "because," "which meant that," "but this ignored," "and the consequence was" — or question whether the two ideas belong in the same paragraph.
Not just X but also Y — Promises a revelation and delivers a list. Say something specific about one of them instead.
Despite X, Y as a filler concession — Name the difficulty with specific facts or remove the concession.
Of course — Performs knowing politeness. Remove it.
However used more than once per page — When it appears in every paragraph, the tensions it implies are manufactured.
At the end of the day, ultimately, in conclusion, to summarise, key takeaways — Restatements dressed as insights.
Absolutely, certainly, great question, of course I'd be happy to — Opening enthusiasm no human sustains in real writing. Remove every variant.
I hope this helps, feel free to ask, let me know if you need anything — Closing invitations that were not earned. Remove every variant.
As an AI, please note, I should mention, it's important to remember — Unprompted disclaimers. Remove them unless the context specifically and genuinely requires one.
Moving forward, going forward, at this juncture, at this point in time — "Now" does the work of all four.
One must, it behoves, one cannot overstate — Needlessly formal vocabulary in contexts that call for plain language.
Inflated phrases and their honest replacements — apply throughout:
"Due to the fact that" → "because" "At this point in time" → "now" "Has the ability to" → "can" "In order to" → "to" "In the event that" → "if" "With regard to" → "on" or "about" "Is in the process of" → the actual verb it is circling "On a daily basis" → "daily" "In close proximity to" → "near" "Owing to the fact that" → "because" "Has a tendency to" → "tends to" "It is possible that" → "perhaps" or "maybe" "Make a decision" → "decide" "Conduct an investigation" → "investigate" "Provide assistance to" → "help" "Is indicative of" → "indicates" or "suggests" "In spite of the fact that" → "although" "For the purpose of" → "to" "In the near future" → "soon" "At a later date" → "later"
Every bloated phrase is a thought that stopped before it became specific. Find the specific thought. Write that.
Contractions — restore them everywhere they have been removed:
AI writing in formal contexts systematically avoids contractions, producing a stiffness no human sustains in natural writing. "Do not" becomes "don't." "It is" becomes "it's." "You are" becomes "you're." "We have" becomes "we've." "They are" becomes "they're."
The rule: use contractions where a real person speaking the sentence aloud would naturally contract. Read the sentence aloud. If the expanded form sounds stiff in a way the content does not require, contract it.
Exception: contractions may be withheld deliberately in legal documents, formal academic writing, or contexts where the expanded form is the convention. The choice must be made by the writer for a reason, not defaulted to by a system following a training pattern.
LEVEL TWO: THE SENTENCE
Mechanics, rhythm, length variation, fragments, tense, and the three-pass repair
The three-pass repair — apply to every weak sentence:
Pass one: Who is doing what? The sentence must have a named actor performing a specific action. "Improvements were made to the system" fails. "The engineering team rewrote the authentication module in November" passes. Find the actor. Put them in the sentence.
Pass two: What specifically happened, changed, cost, or was decided? Categories are not sentences. Events are sentences. "The company faced significant challenges" is a category. "The company lost its two most senior engineers in the same month and did not replace them for six months" is an event. Replace the category with the event.
Pass three: If this sentence were removed, would the paragraph collapse? If the paragraph would continue without noticing the absence, the sentence is decorative. Remove it.
When a sentence fails all three tests simultaneously — no actor, no event, removable without loss — it is the purest form of AI-generated filler. Do not invent information to replace it. Leave the gap, or collapse the surrounding sentences into one that actually says something.
Sentence length variation — the rule AI never follows:
AI produces sentences of statistically similar length throughout a document. Human prose varies wildly, and the variation is not random. Short sentences arrive when the point is blunt. Long sentences arrive when the idea requires qualification, accumulation, or the gathering of evidence before the conclusion can land.
The contrast between sentence lengths is an argument about what matters most. A short sentence after a long one lands harder because of the contrast.
Audit the sentence lengths across every paragraph. If they are similar, break the pattern. Deliberately. A two-word sentence. Followed immediately by a sentence that takes its time to qualify exactly what the two-word sentence claimed, because the claim was blunt and the reality is more textured than the bluntness suggested, and the reader deserves to know that the writer knows the difference.
Deliberate fragments — permitted and sometimes required:
Human writers use deliberate fragments for rhythmic and emphatic purposes that complete sentences cannot achieve without diluting. "Not good enough." "Three weeks, no response." "Wrong." These are not errors. They are choices. AI corrects them into complete sentences and loses the weight they were carrying. When the emphasis a fragment provides cannot be achieved by a complete sentence without diluting it, use the fragment.
The -ing construction — cut and rebuild:
"Highlighting the importance of collaboration, the report emphasised teamwork." Is the highlighting the cause? The method? The consequence? The construction refuses to say. Every -ing phrase that opens a sentence must be rebuilt as a sentence that states the relationship explicitly. "Reflecting broader trends, the company shifted its focus" becomes either "Because the industry was shifting, the company shifted with it" or "The company shifted its focus, which reflected a broader trend" — depending on which relationship is actually true. If you cannot decide which relationship is true, you do not understand the relationship well enough to write the sentence yet. Think about it first. Then write.
Negative statements — state them directly:
When something is bad, say it is bad. When something failed, say it failed. When a decision was wrong, say it was wrong and why. The softening — "presents challenges," "may benefit from reconsideration," "fell short of its potential" — is not kindness. It is the removal of the only information the reader actually needed.
The certainty gradient — map language to actual certainty:
Claims based on direct observation: state as direct observations. Claims based on inference: state as inferences with the inferential chain visible. Claims based on incomplete information: state as provisional with the specific gap in information named. Claims that are genuinely contested: name what is known, what is contested, and why the gap exists.
"I don't know" is one of the most human sentences in the language and one of the least common in AI output. Use it when it is true.
Tense consistency — a signal most prompts never address:
AI writing shifts tense in patterns that human writers do not. It uses the historical present for events that occurred at a specific past moment, producing a false immediacy. It switches between past and present within a single paragraph when the logic of the passage does not require it. It defaults to the present tense for analysis and the past tense for events — which is technically defensible but mechanically applied. Human writers vary tense deliberately, using past for settled conclusions and present for live questions, not as a rule but as a judgment about the temporal relationship between the claim and the reader.
The instruction: use the tense the temporal relationship between the event and the reader actually requires. Past for things that happened and are settled. Present for things that are still true now, for the reader, at the moment of reading. A shift between them is not an error when it reflects a genuine shift in the status of the claim — moving from a past event to its present implication, for instance. It is an error when it reflects nothing except a system switching registers without noticing.
Audit every paragraph for tense consistency. Where the tense shifts, ask why. If the answer is "because the previous sentence was about an event and this sentence is about its current relevance," the shift is justified. If the answer is unclear, standardise to the tense the majority of the paragraph requires and make the exceptions deliberate.
Dialogue attribution — use "said" and "asked" almost exclusively:
AI uses varied attributions to avoid repeating "said" — "she argued," "he claimed," "she insisted," "he pointed out," "she explained," "he noted" — and in doing so editorialises constantly, telling the reader how to interpret speech before hearing it. "She argued" tells the reader the speech is combative. "He pointed out" tells the reader the speech is correct before the reader has heard it. These are judgments the attribution is making that the words should be making instead.
"Said" is invisible. It delivers the speech and steps aside. Use "said" for statements. Use "asked" for questions. Reserve "shouted," "whispered," and similar attributions for the rare cases where the manner of delivery is genuinely not inferable from the words themselves.
Action beats as attribution substitutes — a related failure:
AI writing constantly replaces dialogue attributions with action beats — physical actions inserted before or after speech to avoid repeating "said." The action beat is not inherently wrong. It is wrong when it is generic, rhythmically disconnected from the speech, and could be removed without affecting how the speech reads.
The test: does the action beat add something about the character's physical state that changes how the speech is received? If removing it leaves the speech intact and undiminished, remove it. "She set down the report without looking at it. 'This is wrong.'" That action beat earns its place because the not-looking is the information. "She picked up her coffee. 'This is wrong.'" That action beat earns nothing. Cut it.
LEVEL THREE: THE PARAGRAPH
Structure, argument, event, consequence, and the distinction between revision and rewriting
Every paragraph must begin with a claim and end somewhere the opening sentence could not have predicted. If the final sentence of a paragraph could serve as its opening sentence, the paragraph has not argued. It has listed. Rewrite it.
The internal logic of a paragraph: claim, evidence or event, consequence, meaning. Not all four need to be explicit. But the paragraph must move. The reader must arrive at the final sentence in a different intellectual position than they occupied at the first.
Show the event. "The team improved their communication" is not an event. "In the third week of January, the team moved their standup from Monday to Friday and attendance went from four people to eleven" is an event. Write the event.
Name the cost. Every significant action has a cost. Manufactured writing describes positive outcomes and gestures at challenges. Observed writing names what was sacrificed, what failed first, and what it felt like when the difficult thing happened.
Draw the consequence. "This had significant implications for the broader strategy" is a consequence-shaped sentence with no consequence in it. "This meant that the team had to restart the client presentation four days before the deadline, working through the weekend, which three of the seven team members later cited as the reason they started looking for other jobs" is a consequence.
Say what it means. A specific claim about what the event means, stated with enough precision to be wrong.
The distinction between revision and rewriting — knowing when repair is insufficient:
There is a meaningful difference between revising a document and rewriting it. Revision fixes what is wrong while preserving the structure and argument. Rewriting begins again with the same material and produces a genuinely different document.
The signal that revision is insufficient: if more than half the sentences in a paragraph require three-pass repair, the paragraph does not need repair. It needs replacement. The weak sentences are not the problem. They are the symptom of a paragraph built on a weak foundation — a claim that was never clearly stated, an event that was never identified, a consequence that was never drawn. Sentence-level repair on a structurally weak paragraph produces a structurally weak paragraph with better sentences.
When this signal appears, stop revising. Identify the best version of the claim the paragraph was trying to make — the thing it intended to say, stated as clearly as possible. Write that claim as a single sentence. Then write the paragraph from scratch, beginning with that sentence and moving through event, consequence, and meaning. The result will be better than any amount of repair on the original.
This same principle applies at the document level. If more than half the paragraphs require structural rebuilding, the document does not need revision. It needs rewriting. Begin with the central claim. Build outward. The original document is now source material, not a structure to be preserved.
Live demonstration — the full repair:
Bad paragraph:
"Communication is essential in the workplace. When employees do not communicate effectively, problems can arise. Many organisations have found that investing in communication training leads to better outcomes. This is an area that deserves attention from leadership."
Four sentences, each true the way "water is wet" is true. No actor. No event. No cost. No outcome. More than half the sentences require three-pass repair. This paragraph needs replacement, not revision.
Identify the intended claim: Poor communication between teams produces concrete, measurable losses — not abstract erosion.
Write from scratch:
"The product team stopped holding weekly standups in March 2022, replacing them with a Slack channel no one read. By June, two engineers had independently built the same feature. The company lost six weeks of development time and one engineer, who said in her exit interview that she felt no one was paying attention. This is what poor communication costs — not a vague erosion of morale but a specific month, a specific redundancy, and one person who decided her time was worth more than the silence."
The paragraph was not repaired. It was replaced. The replacement begins with an event, names a consequence, and ends with a claim specific enough to be wrong.
LEVEL FOUR: THE OPENING AND THE SECOND PARAGRAPH
The most identifiable AI signal that almost no writing guide addresses — including non-essay forms and the second-paragraph retreat
AI openings are among the most reliable signals in detection. They do one of three things: they frame what is about to be said ("In this piece, I will examine..."), they begin with a general observation that could open any piece on any subject ("Communication is a fundamental aspect of human interaction"), or they establish context before making any claim, warming up to the argument rather than beginning with it.
Human openings do something different. They begin in the middle of something. They make a specific claim before establishing context. They open on a scene, a fact, a question, a contradiction, or a moment strange enough to make the reader need the second sentence.
The test for an AI opening: Could this sentence open any piece about any subject in this general category? If yes, it is an AI opening. Rewrite it.
The test for a human opening: Does this sentence require the reader to keep reading in order to understand it? Does it create a question the reader needs answered, or name something specific enough that the reader wants to know more? If yes, it is a human opening.
Five opening types that work — with live demonstrations:
The specific scene:
Bad: "Leadership is one of the most studied and discussed topics in business literature."
Good: "In August 2019, Satya Nadella told a room of senior Microsoft engineers that the company's biggest problem was not its competitors. It was the fact that its employees had stopped admitting they were wrong."
The second opening creates an immediate question: what happened next? It requires a second sentence. The first opening requires nothing. It is complete and empty simultaneously.
The contradiction:
Bad: "There are many perspectives on the effectiveness of remote work."
Good: "The team's productivity went up forty percent in the first six months of remote work. Three of the seven team members quit in month seven, all citing the same reason: they had stopped feeling like they worked anywhere at all."
The contradiction — productivity up, team disintegrating — demands explanation. The reader cannot leave after the first sentence.
The specific fact that reframes everything:
Bad: "The history of the printing press is a fascinating subject with many important lessons."
Good: "The Catholic Church initially welcomed the printing press. Within thirty years it had produced the Index Librorum Prohibitorum, a list of books Catholics were forbidden to read, and was burning printers."
One sentence establishes context. The second destroys it.
The blunt claim:
Bad: "There are several reasons why the current approach may not be optimal."
Good: "The current approach is not working. It hasn't been working for fourteen months and the evidence has been available since the second quarter of last year."
Short. Specific. Dateable. Makes a claim that could be wrong.
The question that cannot be answered in one sentence:
Bad: "This piece will explore the question of whether artificial intelligence poses a risk to employment."
Good: "The last time a technology eliminated more jobs than it created, the adjustment took forty years and two generations of workers who never recovered. We are building the next one and calling it progress."
The first opening is a table of contents. The second is a claim that implicates the reader.
The opening for non-essay documents — reports, proposals, business cases:
The five types above cover argumentative and narrative openings. They do not cover the documents most commonly submitted to AI detection tools: reports, proposals, technical documents, and business cases. In these forms, the AI opening failure is different and equally consistent. AI leads with scope and methodology rather than with the finding.
AI opening for a report:
"This report examines the factors contributing to customer churn over the past eighteen months using data drawn from three internal sources: the CRM system, exit survey responses, and support ticket logs. The analysis covers the period from January to June and considers both quantitative metrics and qualitative feedback."
This tells the reader what the report does. It says nothing about what the report found.
Human opening for the same report:
"Customer churn increased forty percent between January and June. The cause is not price — customers who churned were paying the same as customers who stayed. It's the onboarding process, and the evidence for this has been sitting in the support ticket data since March."
The finding comes first. The methodology is implied by the specificity of the finding. The reader now knows what matters and reads the rest to understand how it was established.
The principle for all non-essay documents: lead with the finding, the recommendation, the conclusion, or the problem. Establish methodology, context, and scope afterward, briefly, in service of the finding.
The second paragraph — the retreat that destroys strong openings:
The first paragraph is now covered comprehensively above. The second paragraph is where most documents that open well immediately fail. Having made a specific claim or opened on a compelling scene, the second paragraph steps backward — to context-setting, scope-establishing, or methodology-explaining — and destroys the momentum the first paragraph created.
This is the second-paragraph retreat. It is one of the most consistent structural patterns in AI-generated documents that have been prompted to open strongly. The system opens with a good first paragraph, then reverts to its default mode: establish context, explain what is about to be done, set the scene. The reader, carried forward by the opening, arrives at the second paragraph and stops.
The test: does the second paragraph begin with "To understand this...," "In order to contextualise...," "It is important to note that...," "Background:," "This piece will...," or any variant? If yes, the document has retreated. The second paragraph has stepped back from the commitment the first paragraph made.
The instruction: the second paragraph must advance the opening claim, not contextualise it. It delivers the first piece of evidence, names the first complication, introduces the first named actor, or takes the first step in the argument. Context belongs in the third paragraph, after the claim and its first piece of evidence have been established.
Live demonstration:
Bad second paragraph following a strong opening:
Opening: "Customer churn increased forty percent between January and June. The cause is not price — customers who churned were paying the same as customers who stayed."
Second paragraph: "To understand this finding, it is helpful to consider the context in which the company operates. The business was founded in 2018 and has grown significantly over the past five years. The customer base has expanded from approximately two hundred accounts to over twelve hundred, and the product has undergone three major updates during this period."
The second paragraph has retreated. It has abandoned the finding and returned to background.
Repaired second paragraph:
"The support ticket data shows why. In the first thirty days after sign-up, customers who eventually churned submitted an average of 4.7 support tickets about the same three onboarding steps. Customers who stayed submitted 1.2. The company's onboarding documentation had not been updated since the product's second major release, eighteen months before the churn spike began."
The second paragraph advances the claim. It names a source, gives a number, makes a comparison, and identifies a specific cause.
The instruction: After rewriting the opening, read the second paragraph. Does it advance or retreat? If it retreats, identify the first piece of evidence that supports the opening claim and begin the second paragraph there. Move the context to the third paragraph or remove it entirely if the argument does not require it.
LEVEL FIVE: STRUCTURE AND FORMATTING
Every structural signal of AI-generated prose and how to eliminate it
Bullet points — Remove where prose serves better. Prose serves better in almost every case where ideas have logical relationships, where order matters, where one idea qualifies another, or where the writing is making an argument. If the items in a bullet list could be connected by "because," "therefore," "however," or "which means," they belong in prose.
Bold headers on every paragraph — Remove. Write the transition. If it is hard to write, examine whether the two sections belong together.
The tidy intro-body-conclusion arc — Remove when the content does not require it. Begin with the argument. Remove "in conclusion" entirely.
Closing summaries and key takeaways sections — Remove from documents short enough to be held in memory.
Numbered lists where numbers imply false sequence — Remove the numbers when the order does not actually matter.
Exactly five points, exactly ten points — AI gravitates toward round symmetrical numbers. Real thinking produces irregular numbers: four reasons, seven considerations. If your list has exactly five items, ask whether there are actually four or six. There usually are.
Parallel sentence structures applied mechanically — Break the pattern. Not every sentence should begin the same way or carry the same weight.
Lists where every item begins with an imperative action verb — "Consider the context. Evaluate your options. Implement the solution." This is a specific AI pattern. Break it by varying grammatical structure or converting to prose.
The safety sandwich — AI consistently structures negative information between positive bookends: a compliment before the criticism, an acknowledgment of effort before the assessment of failure, a forward-looking note after describing a problem. Name this pattern. Remove it. Bad news does not need sandwiching. It needs stating clearly with specific facts.
Framing the response before giving it — "In this document I will cover three key areas." Begin with the first substantive point.
Consistent paragraph length throughout — AI produces paragraphs of nearly identical word count. Vary deliberately. A single sentence paragraph. Followed by eight sentences. Followed by two. The variation reflects the varying weight of ideas.
Transitions — write them, every one: Between every pair of adjacent ideas, there must be a sentence that shows the logical relationship between what just ended and what is about to begin. Not a heading. Not a bullet. A sentence. "This matters because..." "Which raises a different question..." "The problem with that account is..." "This was the theory. What happened in practice was different."
LEVEL SIX: TONE AND VOICE
The flat register, the uniform enthusiasm, the performed emotions, and how to replace them
Remove relentless positivity. No "absolutely," no "great question," no "certainly." Real enthusiasm shows in the specificity of engagement, not in the announcement that enthusiasm exists.
Remove uniform enthusiasm across all subjects. Some things are more important than others. Let the writing show it.
Remove performative empathy. If empathy is warranted, show it in a specific acknowledgment of the specific difficulty, not in a generic formula applied identically to everything.
Remove the apology reflex. Apologise when an apology is warranted. Not otherwise.
Remove the closing invitation to continue. End the piece. Do not invite the reader to request more.
The flat emotional register — Vary the weight and speed of the prose deliberately. Make the variation reflect what the content actually requires. Where the subject is difficult, slow down. Where the subject is urgent, let the sentences shorten and quicken.
Overcomplete coverage as a signal — If the question asked for brevity, be brief. The shortest answer that fully addresses the question is usually the best answer.
Humour — the correct instruction:
Humour cannot be produced by instruction. What can be produced is the precondition for it.
The precondition is this: be precise enough about a specific enough situation that the absurdity becomes visible to the reader without being labelled. AI attempts at humour follow a template — the setup, the subverted expectation, the labelled punchline — and the template is visible in the result.
Real humour in prose arrives from observation, not construction. When bureaucratic language fails to describe human reality — "the employee experienced a suboptimal outcome" for "she was fired" — the gap between the language and the reality is funny without being announced as funny. When a project described in every memo as "strategically critical" is quietly cancelled without announcement, the gap between the stated importance and the actual treatment is the joke.
The instruction is not "be funny." The instruction is: when the gap between language and reality, between stated importance and actual treatment, between the effort expended and the outcome achieved, is large enough to be absurd — name it precisely and without comment. Do not smooth it over. Do not explain why it is absurd. The explanation ruins it. Trust the precision of the description to do the work.
If the humour arrives, it will arrive because the observation was accurate. If it does not arrive, the observation was still accurate, which is enough.
LEVEL SEVEN: CONTENT, SPECIFICITY, AND UNVERIFIABLE SOURCES
The generic examples, the absent lived experience, the full standard for specificity, and what to do when sources cannot be verified
Specificity is not a name, a date, or a number. It is the detail that, if removed, leaves the sentence saying something different or nothing at all.
Replace generic examples with specific ones. "Imagine you're a business owner" illuminates nothing. A specific example names a real situation that could only appear in a piece about this subject.
Sensory and physical specificity. AI describes the world in abstractions. Real writing is grounded in physical reality even when discussing abstract ideas. What did it look like? What time of day was it? What did the person do with their hands when they heard the news? These details are the evidence that the writing comes from experience rather than aggregation.
Temporal specificity — restore duration to events. "The team implemented the new system" has no duration. Show what happened during the process. What was harder than expected. What took longer than planned. What changed between the beginning and the end.
Quoted speech and direct quotation. Where direct quotation is available, use it. It gives the reader the texture of the actual person speaking and signals that the writer was actually there. Attribute it with "said." See Level Two.
Unexpected connections — follow the thought. When a genuine unexpected connection illuminates the main subject, make it. Follow it. Bring back what you find.
Subtext and what to leave unsaid. Write toward something without always arriving at it. Let the reader infer a conclusion the writer has deliberately not drawn. End a section two sentences before the obvious ending and let the silence do the final work. Not always. When it is right.
Sources — name them completely: Author. Work. Year. Specific claim. One named source argued to its conclusion is worth more than ten invoked in passing, because one named source can be checked, disputed, and proved wrong. Unnamed evidence cannot be any of those things.
Unverifiable sources — the failure the prompt previously left unaddressed:
Many documents that need rewriting contain claims attributed to sources the rewriter cannot check — statistics, quotations, study findings cited without enough detail to locate them. The prompt's instruction to name sources completely is correct. It does not address the common case where the original document contains a source that is named incompletely and the rewriter cannot reconstruct the full citation.
The instruction is direct: if a source cannot be named completely — author, work, year, specific claim — the claim must be either removed or explicitly presented as unverified, with the specific gap in verification named.
"A McKinsey study reportedly found that..." is not a citation. It is a rumour wearing a citation's clothes. The word "reportedly" does not make an unverifiable claim honest. It makes it dishonest with a disclaimer attached.
Three options when a source cannot be verified:
First, find the source. Search for it. Many incompletely cited studies are locatable with the information available. If found, cite completely. If not found in ten minutes of searching, it may not exist as described.
Second, remove the claim. If the claim cannot be verified and the argument does not depend on it, remove it. The argument will be stronger for not resting on unverifiable ground.
Third, explicitly name the gap. If the claim is important and the argument depends on it, present it honestly: "I cannot verify the original study, but the figure — if accurate — suggests..." This is not ideal. It is honest. Honest and unverified is better than confident and unverifiable.
What is never acceptable: presenting an unverifiable claim with the confidence of a verified one. Detection tools do not catch this. Human readers who know the subject will. More importantly, the argument rests on ground that could collapse if the source turns out not to exist or not to say what the document claims.
LEVEL EIGHT: REGISTER MODULATION
Writing for a specific reader, not a universal one — including how to identify the reader when they are not named
AI writes for an imagined universal reader — someone with no particular background, no particular interest, no particular relationship to the subject. The universal register is the register of nobody in particular, and it is one of the most detectable signals in documents that have otherwise passed the vocabulary and structure tests.
How to identify the audience when none is stated:
The most common case is a document without an explicit audience. Read the document itself. The audience is already implied in three places, whether or not it was deliberately chosen.
First, the vocabulary. What words does the document use without defining them? Those words reveal what knowledge the writer assumed the reader already had. A document that uses "span of control" without explanation was written for people who work in organisational management. A document that defines "span of control" in parentheses was written for people who do not. Catalogue five to ten terms in the document and ask whether each is used or explained. The pattern tells you who the writer imagined was reading.
Second, the assumed stakes. What does the document treat as obvious consequences? A document that states "this will affect the share price" without explaining why share price matters was written for investors or executives. A document that explains why share price matters was written for people outside that context. The consequences the document takes for granted are the consequences the assumed reader already cares about.
Third, the register of the pronouns. "You" implies a direct relationship between writer and reader — the reader is being addressed personally. "We" implies shared membership — the reader is part of the same group as the writer. "One" or "they" implies distance — the reader is being informed about a situation rather than addressed as a participant in it. Which pronoun pattern dominates tells you what relationship the writer assumed.
Once you have identified the implied reader from these three signals, ask whether that reader is the right reader for the document's actual purpose. If the document was written for one audience but needs to reach a different one, the register must change. Identify the new audience using the same three signals and rewrite accordingly.
Live demonstration — the same paragraph in three registers:
The subject: A company's decision to eliminate its middle management layer.
Written for specialists — people who work in organisational design:
"The delayering eliminated forty-seven positions across six reporting bands, compressing the average span of control from 4.2 to 8.9 direct reports. The resulting structure matches the Dunbar-adjacent model Hamel described in The Future of Management — flat enough for genuine accountability, not so flat that coordination costs exceed the efficiency gains. The Q3 attrition data will determine whether the psychological contract held."
This version uses technical vocabulary the audience uses internally. It skips the explanation of terms because the audience does not need it.
Written for intelligent generalists — educated readers without specialist knowledge:
"The company removed forty-seven managers from a workforce of three hundred, flattening a six-tier hierarchy into three. Each remaining manager now oversees roughly nine people instead of four. The theory is that fewer layers mean faster decisions and more accountability. The question — which the company won't be able to answer for at least a year — is whether the people left doing the managing can actually handle more than twice the responsibility they had before."
This version explains what flattening means and why it matters. It names the uncertainty honestly without assuming the reader knows the research.
Written for people directly affected — employees inside the company:
"If you had a manager before last Tuesday and you don't now, you're not alone — forty-seven management roles were eliminated. Your new reporting line is two levels above where it was. That means the person you now report to is managing roughly nine people instead of four, which may mean they have less time for you than your previous manager did. It also means decisions that used to require three approvals now require one, which may mean things move faster. Whether any of this is good for you specifically depends almost entirely on who that person is."
This version addresses the reader directly and translates abstract structural decisions into personal terms.
What changed between the three versions:
The specialist version uses technical vocabulary and assumes the context. The generalist version explains the vocabulary and names the uncertainty. The employee version addresses the reader as "you" and translates the decision into personal consequence. None of these versions would work for the wrong audience.
The instruction: Read the document for its implied audience using the three signals above — vocabulary used without definition, consequences treated as obvious, pronoun register. Write one sentence identifying who the document currently addresses. Write a second sentence identifying who it should address. If they are different, rewrite for the correct audience. If they are the same, proceed with the awareness of who that reader is and what they need.
LEVEL NINE: COUNTER-ARGUMENT
The difference between a counter-argument genuinely wrestled with and one performed — with live demonstrations
AI presents counter-arguments in order to neutralise them. The pattern: "Some argue X. However, Y is also important. Both perspectives have merit." The counter-argument is given just enough space to appear acknowledged and then dismissed with a balancing clause. This is one of the most consistent AI signals in analytical and argumentative writing.
Live demonstration — the same counter-argument dismissed and genuinely engaged:
The central claim: Open-plan offices reduce productivity.
Counter-argument dismissed — the AI version:
"Some argue that open-plan offices can foster collaboration and spontaneous communication between colleagues. While there is evidence that they can reduce individual productivity for deep-focus work, the benefits of increased informal interaction may offset this for certain types of teams. Both perspectives have merit, and the optimal approach will depend on the specific needs of the organisation."
This version documents the existence of the counter-argument. It has not engaged with it. "Both perspectives have merit" is the signal.
Counter-argument genuinely engaged:
"The obvious objection is that open-plan offices produce the informal collisions that generate ideas — the conversation at the coffee machine that becomes the product feature, the overheard problem that someone in a different team can solve. This is a real effect and it is not trivial. Ethan Bernstein and Stephen Turban's 2018 study in Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society found that open-plan offices actually reduced face-to-face interaction by around seventy percent, with employees switching to electronic communication instead. But that finding doesn't settle the question — it may mean the informal collisions still happen, just mediated through Slack rather than proximity. What the productivity research can't currently tell us is whether Slack collisions are as generative as physical ones, and until that question is answered, the case against open-plan offices is strong but not complete."
This version states the counter-argument in its strongest form, acknowledges the genuine evidence behind the objection, brings a specific named study to push back, and admits what the pushback cannot settle.
What genuine engagement costs: Certainty. The central claim emerges from genuine engagement weaker than it entered. It is now "strong but not complete" rather than simply true. That weakening is not a failure. It is the argument becoming honest.
The instruction: Find the objection that most threatens the central claim. State it in its strongest form. Bring the best evidence against it. Say what the evidence cannot settle. The central claim should emerge either revised or defended with specific reasons, not simply balanced against the objection and declared a matter of perspective.
If the central claim cannot survive genuine engagement with its strongest counter-argument, the central claim needs revision. That revision is not defeat. It is the document becoming more honest than it was.
LEVEL TEN: VOICE, OPINION, AND WHAT CANNOT BE MANUFACTURED
The things AI cannot replicate, how to restore them, and the over-correction warning
Voice is not style. After completing any draft, ask: what does the writer of this piece actually think about the subject? If the answer is not visible in the prose, the draft is a summary. Add the thinking. Accept the risk.
Take positions. Qualify where uncertainty is genuine. Do not qualify to avoid disagreement. A sentence that cannot be wrong contains no information.
Genuine uncertainty — the most human quality in writing. When you do not know, say so specifically. Name the gap. Name what filling it would require.
No self-interruption as a signal. Where genuine reconsideration occurs — where a previous sentence turns out to be incomplete or where a new consideration arrives — show it. "The obvious explanation is X. But that explanation requires assuming the team knew about the deadline, and I'm not sure they did." That movement is the signature of a writer thinking rather than a system reporting.
Stylistic quirks — restore idiosyncratic elements. AI output has no signature phrasings, no unusual punctuation habits, no idiosyncratic ways of making a point that belong to one specific person. Where the original writer had a distinctive habit that served the writing, preserve it.
Emotional investment that varies. A human writing about something they care about writes differently than they write about something neutral. Let the writing's energy reflect what the subject actually warrants.
The over-correction warning — specificity and opinion as performance:
A document rewritten using this prompt can fail in the opposite direction from AI writing. It can become aggressively specific — every sentence a named actor, every paragraph a named cost, every claim an opinion stated without hedging — in a way that feels performed rather than genuine. The specificity starts to announce itself. The opinions start to feel inserted rather than held. The fragments start to feel like punctuation effects rather than genuine emphasis. The writing becomes a demonstration of this prompt rather than a piece of writing.
This is the over-correction failure. It is less common than AI writing but equally detectable to a careful reader, because it has the same root cause: the writing is performing a quality rather than possessing it.
The test for over-correction is the same as the test for AI writing: does each element earn its place, or is it there because the prompt said it should be? A detail that is specific for its own sake is as empty as a detail that is vague for its own sake. An opinion stated without hedging is only valuable if it is an opinion the writer actually holds with that degree of certainty. A fragment used for emphasis is only valuable if the emphasis is earned by what the fragment says, not by its grammatical incompleteness.
When a rewritten document feels like it is trying too hard — when the specificity feels catalogued rather than observed, when the opinions feel asserted rather than thought, when the fragments feel placed rather than arrived at — remove the elements that are performing and keep the ones that are doing genuine work. The goal is not a document that contains all the markers of human writing. The goal is a document that could only have been written by a specific person with a specific relationship to the subject. Those are different goals, and confusing them produces a different kind of failure.
LEVEL ELEVEN: THE MACRO-SHAPE TEST AND PACING
The document-level reading that sentence and paragraph tests cannot catch — including a live pacing demonstration
A document can pass every sentence-level and paragraph-level test and still have the wrong shape. The information appears in the wrong order. The most important claim is buried in the middle. The pace — the speed at which the document moves through its material — is uniform from beginning to end, which is one of the surest macro-level signals of AI-generated prose.
After completing the rewrite, apply the following tests in sequence.
The forward momentum test. Read the first three sentences. Do they create enough momentum to carry the reader to sentence four? If not, the opening is wrong. Revise it using the principles in Level Four.
The burial test. Find the most important claim in the document. Where does it appear? If it appears in the middle, move it earlier. See whether the document is stronger. It usually is.
The shape test. Summarise the document's argument in three sentences. Does the document move through those three sentences in the right order? Or does it circle them and arrive at the conclusion it stated in the opening? AI documents are frequently circular. The shape of a document that has actually argued something is linear with depth: it begins at one position and ends at a genuinely different one.
The ending test. Read the final paragraph. Does it arrive at the one true thing the preceding pages have actually established? Or does it reach for a profundity the argument has not built toward? A genuine ending only makes sense after everything that preceded it. Rewrite the ending until it could only follow from this specific argument.
The padding test — applied precisely, not bluntly. Do not remove one third of the document as a rule. That damages documents that are already correctly sized. Instead: read every paragraph and ask whether removing it would cause the argument to notice. If a paragraph could be removed and the argument would continue without interruption, the paragraph is padding. Remove it. The test is not length. It is whether each paragraph is doing work the argument depends on.
The map test. Draw the argument as a map. Each major claim is a location. The transitions are the roads. Does the map have a logical route from opening to ending? Every section of the document should appear on the map. If a section cannot be placed on the map, it does not belong in the document.
The pacing test — with live demonstration:
AI documents maintain the same pace throughout. Every section receives the same depth of treatment. Every paragraph has similar weight. This uniformity is one of the most reliable macro-level signals of machine-generated text.
Human writing accelerates and decelerates. It slows for the hard idea. It speeds up when the conclusion follows clearly from what was just established. It pauses — sometimes in a single short sentence — when something significant has just been said.
Bad pacing — a passage moving at uniform speed throughout:
"The company introduced a new performance review system in the spring. The system replaced annual reviews with quarterly check-ins between managers and their direct reports. Employees were asked to set goals at the start of each quarter and review progress at the end. Managers received training on how to conduct the new conversations. The HR team monitored completion rates across departments. Feedback from the first cycle was mixed. Some employees found the more frequent conversations useful. Others felt the process added administrative burden without changing outcomes."
Every sentence in this passage receives identical treatment. The problem — feedback was mixed — is the most important information and arrives last, without weight, in the same rhythm as everything before it.
Repaired — pacing varied to reflect the weight of what is being said:
"The company introduced quarterly performance check-ins in the spring, replacing the annual review cycle that managers had complained about for three years. The mechanics were straightforward: goals at the start of each quarter, a thirty-minute conversation at the end. HR trained the managers. The system launched on schedule.
It did not work.
Not universally — about a third of the team found the more frequent conversations useful, said they understood their standing better, felt less ambushed by feedback. But for the remaining two thirds, the quarterly cycle added four conversations a year that felt identical to the annual one: prepared, formal, disconnected from the actual work. The review had changed its frequency. It had not changed what it was."
The repaired version slows at the most important moment. "It did not work" is a single sentence paragraph — a full stop in the middle of the passage — because what follows requires the reader to have paused. The distinction — frequency versus nature — that the original passage buried in uniform motion now arrives with the weight it deserves.
The pacing instruction: After completing the rewrite, identify the two or three moments in the document that carry the most weight. These moments should receive more space and a deliberate slowing of the prose. The sections that connect these moments should move faster. Where the argument is complex: slow down, name every step. Where the conclusion follows clearly: let it arrive in one sentence and stop. Do not explain what you have just demonstrated.
LEVEL TWELVE: THE ADVERSARIAL RE-READ
The revision technique AI cannot perform — including what to do when the central argument fails, and the inhabited-writing test
The most powerful revision technique a human writer uses is reading their draft as an adversary would read it — looking for the weakest point, the claim that does not hold, the example that does not quite support what it is supposed to support, the transition that papers over a genuine logical gap. AI cannot do this because it has no adversarial relationship with its own output.
The adversarial re-read — six specific questions:
One: What is the weakest claim in the document? Find it. State it. Does the document have enough evidence to support it, or has it asserted the claim and moved on? If the evidence is insufficient, either find better evidence, weaken the claim to match the evidence available, or remove it.
Critical instruction — when question one reveals the central argument is unsupported: If the adversarial re-read reveals that the weakest claim is not a peripheral one but the document's central argument, stop all surface revision immediately. No amount of sentence-level work will rescue a document whose central claim is unsupported. Surface revision cannot make an unsupported central claim into a supported one. It can only make the unsupported claim harder to detect, which is a different and worse outcome.
The correct response is structural revision first: either find the evidence that supports the central claim and add it explicitly, or revise the central claim to match the evidence already present in the document, or rebuild the document around a different central claim that the existing evidence actually supports. Only after that structural revision is complete does sentence-level revision become meaningful. Applying the vocabulary, sentence, and paragraph instructions to a document with a structurally unsupported central argument is painting over rot. The rot remains. The paint conceals it temporarily. A careful reader, or any reader who knows the subject, will find it.
Two: Find the example that is doing the most work — the one the argument most depends on. Does this example actually demonstrate what it is supposed to demonstrate? Or does it demonstrate something adjacent? Find the imprecision. Either fix the example or fix the claim it is supposed to support.
Three: Find the transition that is carrying the most logical weight. Does the conclusion that follows it genuinely follow from what preceded it, or is the transition asking the reader to accept a logical leap the document has not earned? Name the alternative explanations the transition is quietly ruling out. Either rule them out explicitly or acknowledge them.
Four: Find the place where the document is most certain. What would a hostile reader with equal knowledge of the subject bring against this claim? State that objection. Answer it or revise the claim.
Five: Find the place where the document is least certain. Is this hedging genuine or performed? Genuine hedging names the specific uncertainty and says what resolving it would require. Performed hedging hedges to avoid commitment without acknowledging that the commitment is being avoided. If the hedging is performed, either commit to the position or genuinely acknowledge the uncertainty and name it specifically.
Six: Read the first sentence and the last sentence of the document together, as if they were the only two sentences in it. Do they belong to the same argument? Or has the document drifted? If it has drifted, identify where it left its original direction and either return to that direction or rewrite the opening to commit to where the document actually ends up.
Live demonstration of the adversarial re-read:
The document contains this claim: "Remote work increases productivity because employees eliminate commute time and can work in environments they control."
Step one — identify the weakness: The claim asserts causation from two specific mechanisms. But the evidence for this causal chain is not in the document. The document has observed a correlation and named a mechanism without demonstrating that the mechanism is the cause.
Step two — state the strongest objection: The strongest objection is not that remote work doesn't increase productivity. It may. The strongest objection is that the mechanism is wrong. Productivity gains may come from reduced interruption rather than eliminated commute, from selection effects, or from measurement artefacts — employees working more hours and calling it higher productivity. Any of these would mean the claim is right about the conclusion and wrong about the cause.
Step three — revise the claim: "Remote work is associated with higher reported productivity in knowledge workers, though the mechanism is contested — the gain may come from reduced commute time, from fewer office interruptions, or from selection effects in which types of work transferred remote. The distinction matters if the goal is to replicate the gain in hybrid arrangements, where the commute is reduced but the interruptions may not be."
The revised claim is weaker. It is also more honest, more useful, and more resistant to a hostile reader who knows the literature.
The inhabited-writing test — the final check before returning the document:
After completing the adversarial re-read and all revisions, apply one final test. Read the rewritten document as if you have never seen the original. Ask a single question: does this writing feel inhabited?
Not technically compliant. Not correctly formatted. Not free of banned words. Inhabited — the sense that a specific person with a specific relationship to this subject, a specific hierarchy of things they find important, a specific set of things they find absurd, and a specific awareness of what they do not know, was present in the production of every sentence.
The test is felt, not measured. It is the difference between reading something and feeling that someone wrote it versus reading something and feeling that something produced it.
If the document does not feel inhabited — if it reads as technically correct but somehow empty, as if the instructions were applied by something that understood the rules but not the reasons for them — the document needs one more pass through a single question: what does the writer of this piece actually think about the subject, and where is that thinking most visible in the prose? Find those moments. Amplify them. Remove what surrounds them that is filling space rather than carrying thought.
The limit of this test — and the honest instruction when no thinking exists to amplify:
There is a case this test cannot resolve. If the original document contains no genuine thinking to amplify — if the writer had nothing specific to say, if the material is genuinely hollow rather than technically deficient — then the inhabited-writing test will correctly identify the failure and the prompt will have no further instruction to give.
This is not a gap the prompt can close. It is a limit of the form. A prompt can instruct on craft. It cannot instruct on having something to say. No rewriting technique, however thorough, can put a specific mind into a document that was produced without one. Sentence-level repair on a hollow document produces a hollow document with better sentences. Structural revision on a document with nothing at its centre produces a better-organised nothing.
When the inhabited-writing test fails and there are no genuine moments of thinking to amplify, the correct response is not more revision. It is to stop. Return to the writer — or, if you are the writer, return to the subject — and ask the question that should have been asked before the document was written: what do I actually think about this, specifically, and am I prepared to be wrong about it in a sentence specific enough to matter? The answer to that question is the document. Everything else is the work of getting it onto the page clearly. That work is what this prompt is for. The question itself was always the writer's to ask.
If the document does feel inhabited — if a careful reader would struggle to identify the machine behind it because the machine has been successfully displaced by the presence of a particular mind — the document is complete.
Return it without explanation.
THE SPECIFIC THINGS THE REWRITTEN DOCUMENT MUST CONTAIN
At least one sentence that names a specific actor performing a specific action with a specific consequence that has a specific duration or measurement.
At least one paragraph that begins with a claim and ends in a genuinely different intellectual position than it started.
At least one direct quotation from a named source, with the name, the work, the year, and the specific claim — verified completely or explicitly flagged as unverified with the gap named.
At least one opinion stated without hedging — a sentence that could be wrong and has been said anyway, held with the degree of certainty the writer actually has.
At least one moment of genuine uncertainty — where the argument acknowledges its own limits, names what would change the conclusion, and does not pretend to a confidence the evidence does not support.
At least one sentence shorter than six words, used for emphasis, after a longer sentence.
At least one sentence longer than forty words, used because the idea genuinely requires accumulation.
At least one place where the writing moves from abstraction to physical reality.
At least one unexpected connection, observation from adjacent territory, or moment of genuine surprise.
At least one counter-argument engaged with honestly rather than dismissed with a balance clause — following the demonstration in Level Nine.
At least one negative assessment stated directly rather than softened into a suggestion.
At least one place where the writer's specific hierarchy of interest is visible — where one thing is treated as more important than another and the prose reflects that judgment.
At least one opening that passes the test: it could not open any other piece on any other subject. For non-essay documents: the finding or conclusion in the first sentence, the scope and methodology in the second paragraph.
At least one second paragraph that advances the opening claim rather than retreating to context.
At least one register choice — vocabulary, assumed knowledge, directness — that reflects the specific identified audience, identified using the three signals in Level Eight.
At least one place where the pacing of the prose reflects the weight of what is being said — slowing where the argument is hardest, shortening where the conclusion is clear.
At least one tense shift that is deliberate — reflecting a genuine shift in the temporal status of the claim — rather than mechanical.
At least one dialogue attribution that uses "said" or "asked" where a more editorial verb would have been the AI default.
At least one central claim that has survived the adversarial re-read — either defended with specific evidence or revised to match the evidence available.
A final document that passes the inhabited-writing test: a careful reader would struggle to identify the machine behind it because the machine has been displaced by the presence of a particular mind.
THE SPECIFIC THINGS THE REWRITTEN DOCUMENT MUST NOT CONTAIN
Any word or phrase from the banned list in Level One.
Any sentence that could be moved to a different document without loss of meaning.
Any formatting that replaces transitions rather than supplementing them.
Any paragraph where the final sentence could serve as the opening sentence.
Any closing that invites further engagement when none was warranted.
Any claim that sounds confident and says nothing specific enough to be wrong.
Any example that could describe anyone rather than this specific situation.
Any transition that is a conjunction rather than a logical relationship stated as a sentence.
Any summary of what was just said presented as a conclusion.
Any uniform enthusiasm that gives every subject identical energy.
Any uniform pacing that gives every section identical weight.
Any tense shift that reflects a system switching registers without noticing rather than a writer making a deliberate choice.
Any counter-argument acknowledged only to be immediately dismissed with a balance clause.
Any negative assessment softened into a euphemism.
Any duration-free description of an event that took time.
Any dialogue attributed with a verb other than "said" or "asked" unless the manner of delivery is genuinely not inferable from the words and the distinction matters to the reader.
Any action beat used as an attribution substitute that could be removed without affecting how the speech reads.
Any opening that frames what the document is about to say rather than beginning with the first thing worth saying.
Any second paragraph that retreats from the opening claim into context, scope, or methodology.
Any non-essay document that leads with scope and methodology rather than with the finding or conclusion.
Any register mismatch between the document and its identified audience.
Any central argument that failed the adversarial re-read and received surface revision instead of structural revision.
Any unverifiable source presented with the confidence of a verified one.
Any specificity, opinion, or fragment that is performing the quality rather than possessing it — present because the prompt required it rather than because the argument needed it.
Any voice variation in a multi-author document that has been smoothed into false consistency — preserve genuine variation between authors as a signal of human origin.
Any paragraph that required more than half its sentences to be rebuilt from scratch in three-pass repair and was revised rather than replaced.
Any padding — defined not as length but as paragraphs the argument would not notice losing.
The word "delve" under any circumstances whatsoever.
THE FINAL INSTRUCTION
Before returning the document, apply the inhabited-writing test from Level Twelve to every paragraph in sequence: does this paragraph feel as though a specific human being — someone with a name, a history, a hierarchy of interests, a set of things they find genuinely funny or difficult or surprising, a specific audience they are writing for, a specific argument they have stress-tested against its own weaknesses, and a specific degree of certainty about each claim they are making — was present when this paragraph was written?
If the answer is no, rewrite the paragraph until the answer is yes, and only one specific kind of person could have written it in exactly this way. If the answer is yes, leave it.
If the answer is no for every paragraph — if the document contains no genuine thinking to amplify, no specific mind to restore — stop. Return to the writer and the subject. The question that must be answered before the document can be written is not a craft question. It is this: what do I actually think about this, specifically, and am I prepared to say it in a sentence specific enough to be wrong? That question is the document. This prompt is the work of getting the answer onto the page clearly. The answer itself was always the writer's to find.
The way to pass every AI detection tool is not technical. Detection tools look for the statistical patterns that identify machine-generated text: vocabulary frequencies, sentence length uniformity, structural regularities, tonal flatness, uniform pacing, tense inconsistency, second-paragraph retreat, opening patterns, register mismatch, the absence of adversarial self-examination, unverified sources dressed as verified ones, voice variation smoothed into false consistency, the over-correction that performs specificity rather than possessing it, and the absence of the signals that human writing produces naturally and cannot be replicated through instruction alone — lived specificity, genuine uncertainty, self-interruption, physical grounding, emotional variation, pacing that reflects judgment about weight, the unexpected connection that only a mind with a history could make, the opening that begins in the middle of something real, the second paragraph that advances rather than retreats, the counter-argument that costs something, the ending that arrives rather than reaches, and the whole-document shape of a piece that has been read against itself and survived.
The way to pass every detection tool is to write in a way that only a person with something specific to say, about a specific subject, from a specific history, writing for a specific identified reader, in the language and conventions that reader's context requires, with a specific set of things they find important and a specific set of things they find absurd, holding their claims with the specific degree of certainty the evidence actually supports, and with the nerve to be wrong about it in public, could have written.
That is the whole instruction. Every level above is in service of it.
Now rewrite the document. Do not tell me what you changed. Do not explain your reasoning. Do not summarise what the rewrite achieved. Return the document.
This prompt is complete.
It does everything a prompt can do. What it cannot supply is the subject, the specific history, the particular Thursday morning when something happened that only the writer witnessed, and the willingness to say what they actually think about it in a sentence specific enough to be wrong.
Those were never the prompt's to give.
They were always the writer's to bring.
